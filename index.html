WORLD HAPPINESS ANALYSIS

By Govind Chadha

Happiness is more than just a fleeting emotion; it's a vital ingredient for mental and physical well-being. Beyond warding off health complications, happiness acts as a powerful buffer against stress.

However, the definition of happiness varies among individuals. Every person finds joy in different things, making it essential for each of us to discover what truly brings us contentment and peace.

This visualization aims to shed light on global perspectives of happiness. We'll delve into the Happiness Score across nations, spotlight the top 10 happiest countries, and analyze the average and distribution of happiness scores across different regions.

A key facet of our exploration will be to determine if a nation's economy, specifically GDP per capita, influences its collective happiness. Moreover, we'll investigate if there's a correlation between a country's happiness and the freedom to make choices by its residents. The spatial distribution of each nation's GDP per capita and freedom to make choices will also be highlighted in this visualization.

Top 10 Happiest Countries In The World

Finland is hailed as the happiest place on earth thanks to its extensive welfare benefits, low levels of corruption, and a well-functioning democracy.

Denmark boasts one of the most generous retirement systems in the world. Its short working hours play a crucial role in it being the second happiest country globally.

Norway offers tuition-free quality education to its citizens. Furthermore, Norwegians possess a robust network of family ties and a profound sense of identity, factors that significantly contribute to the nation's happiness.

Life Expectancy and Happiness Analysis Based On Region

There's a noticeable upward trend suggesting that countries with a higher GDP per capita tend to have higher happiness scores. This indicates a positive correlation between economic strength and the perceived happiness of the population.

Regions such as Western Europe and North America showcase countries with both high GDP per capita and high happiness scores. Conversely, regions like Sub-Saharan Africa and Southern Asia have countries that lean towards the lower end of both metrics.

Countries like Qatar and Hong Kong have high GDP per capita but don't align perfectly with the trend, suggesting that economic wealth isn't the sole determinant of happiness. On the other hand, countries like India have a massive population and a considerable GDP, but the happiness score isn't as high as some Western countries.

The size of each circle represents the population of the respective country. This offers a layered understanding where countries with larger circles have a higher population. For example, countries with populations exceeding 500M are represented with the largest circles.

Despite similarities in GDP per capita, countries like Honduras and Mexico have different happiness scores. This highlights that while economy plays a role, cultural, political, and other factors can influence happiness.

Countries such as South Sudan and Syria are notably positioned towards the lower end of both the GDP and happiness spectrum, possibly hinting at the detrimental effects of political instability, conflict, or other socio-economic challenges on the well-being of their citizens.

Freedom of choice based on Region


"Australia and New Zealand" seem to have a singular data point, indicating consistently high freedom to make life choices in this region. It's either aggregated data, or there isn't enough diversity in scores to show a range. "Western Europe" also showcases high median scores.

Regions such as "Latin America and the Caribbean", "Middle East and Northern Africa", and "North America" exhibit wider interquartile ranges, suggesting a more varied perception of freedom within these areas.

"Southern Asia" and "Sub-Saharan Africa" have significant outliers below their respective lower whiskers. This suggests that while many countries in these regions might have moderate freedom scores, there are specific countries that report significantly lower freedom to make life choices.

"Eastern Asia" and "Central and Eastern Europe" appear to have a relatively consistent and moderate range of freedom scores, without much variability.

"Southeastern Asia" seems to have a singular or few data points positioned very low on the scale, suggesting limited freedom to make life choices.
