GLOBAL ECONOMY ANALYSIS

By Rounak Agarwal

Economic indicators are statistics that are used to obtain a more precise picture of the state of the economy today, as their name suggests. Few pertinent examples include the Gross Domestic Product (GDP), the Unemployment Rate, Trade, and Globalisation. When anticipating how the economy will develop over the coming years, economic data and their interpretation by reliable sources are crucial.

With the help of these forecasts, both consumers and businesses will be able to make informed choices about their long-term financial plans, including where and how much money to invest, whether to hire more people or cut back, whether to buy real estate, etc.

Famous Indicators of Economic Growth

The Gross Domestic Product (GDP) is a common statistic used by countries around the world to assess how well they are doing economically. GDP is a gauge of how much money has been made through time from the production of products and services. The GDP of a nation is widely used to gauge economic health and determine whether the economy is growing or experiencing a recession.

The COVID-19 virus grew alarmingly in 2020, sickening millions of people and halting economic activity as nations implemented tight travel restrictions to stop the infection's spread. The world's largest economic shock in decades has already occurred, and the economic damage is evident as the health and human toll mounts. Virtually every nation's GDP rate has been impacted by the virus, and a 5.2 percent decline in the global GDP rate is anticipated in 2020. It is considered as the worst global recession in decades, despite unprecedented efforts by governments to counteract the downturn through fiscal and monetary policy support.

Unemployment refers to those who are available to work but are still unable to locate work, and it is utilized as an economic indicator. The unemployment rate is a significant issue for governments, as unemployed people do not pay taxes but instead get benefits. As a result, the government's finances are harmed by high unemployment, resulting in increased public borrowing.
